## First: what a hydrogen bomb is (conceptually)

A hydrogen bomb (thermonuclear weapon) releases energy using **nuclear fusion**, the same process that powers stars.

* **Fission** = splitting heavy atoms (like uranium)
* **Fusion** = combining light atoms (like hydrogen isotopes)

Fusion releases *far more energy per unit mass* than fission—but only under extreme conditions.

---

## Why fusion is hard to achieve

Hydrogen nuclei are **positively charged**, so they repel each other.
To force them to fuse, you need:

* **Extremely high temperature** (tens of millions of degrees)
* **Extremely high pressure**

On Earth, those conditions do not occur naturally.

---

## The key idea (without construction details)

The central concept is:

> A **fission explosion** is used as an **energy source** to create the conditions needed for **fusion**.

Important distinction:

* This does **not** mean “two bombs taped together”
* It means one nuclear reaction is used to *trigger* another, much more powerful one

---

## High-level sequence (physics only)

1. **Initial energy release**

   * A nuclear fission reaction produces an enormous burst of energy.

2. **Compression and heating**

   * That energy creates temperatures and pressures high enough for fusion to occur.

3. **Fusion reaction**

   * Light hydrogen nuclei combine into heavier nuclei.
   * A small amount of mass converts directly into energy (`E = mc²`).

4. **Energy amplification**

   * Fusion releases fast neutrons that can cause additional reactions.
   * This dramatically multiplies the total energy output.

This is why thermonuclear weapons can be **orders of magnitude** more powerful than fission bombs.

---

## Why this is not “just bigger explosives”

Key differences from conventional weapons:

* Energy comes from **nuclear binding energy**, not chemistry
* Output is not limited by explosive material volume
* Scale can be increased far beyond fission alone
* Effects include intense radiation, thermal energy, and shockwaves
